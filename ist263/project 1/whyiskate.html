Why trust my advice?

A fair question, but I've been skating for a good while now.

My first custom deck

After having skated for close to 6 years now, I’m still not very good. I can do one slide with gloves, can confidently get myself from A to B, and can manage hills OK. I skate almost every day during the school season and typically only take a spill a couple of times a year. But I’m happy with where I am. Unlike a lot of sports, its something you can take at your own pace and comfort level. There’s never anyone egging you to go beyond your limits or to do something dangerous. Skating gives you a similar level of freedom to biking without the encumbering size, to roller blading/skating without the time-consuming lacing, to roller skiing without looking like a total nerd. It’s a far more pleasant way to get around than using a car, and there’s no better feeling than having the freedom to get anywhere in a mile radius in just a couple of minutes without having to start a combustion engine.

Former Fool's Corner

I thought longboarding was really dumb when I first heard about it in high school. When I was a little kid, I tried skateboarding because my best friend was really invested in it. I didn’t have the patience and my tiny childhood skateboard went unused until it was lost in moving houses. I teased my friends in high school that longboarding is just skateboarding (which, it is, but its also different) until after a year of putting it off and excluding myself I gave it a shot and slowly fell in love. In 10th grade my friend lent me his sturdy Arbor board for a couple of weeks. I didn’t even have a skate tool to adjust how tight the trucks were, but every day after school I still walked down my hill and practiced skating in a very straight line down the Route that turned into a local road upon entering town. As I got older, I stopped being an outdoorsy kid, but longboarding gave me a reason to spend afternoons outside again.

Eventually I was enamored to the point where I had to get a board for myself, so I made the trek with my mom to the closest skateboard-adjacent store: a Zumiez in a mall an hour away where I overpaid for a Sector Nine double-kick drop-through board. For about two years that was my only board, and I didn’t buy my second until just before I went away to college. I got my third board, which looked like the first except a couple of inches shorter and covered on the bottom with a cheap sticker design, by winning a contest at the end of my freshman year. My fourth was a large cruiser I got my first semester in Syracuse. I bought it at the very end of fall, so I had to wait an entire winter before I really got to use it.

Me and that board are on somewhat bad terms because it’s the one I’ve fallen off far more times than any other. And recently I purchased a real honest to god skateboard that I haven’t had time to learn to ride—I really didn’t expect how different it would feel though. It’s almost like starting from scratch.
